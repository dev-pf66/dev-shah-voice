## Post 3: Deal Sourcing Tactics That Actually Work

**Type:** Tactical / How-To
**Length:** ~450 words

---

847 deals. 4 closed.

Here's what I learned about deal sourcing:

"Build relationships" is advice for people with 5 years to wait.

I had 6 months.

What actually worked:

**1. Outbound > Inbound**

Acquire.com, BizBuySell—everyone's fishing there.
2,000 buyers per listing.

We sent 200 cold emails/week to owners NOT listing.
Response rate: 12%.
Closed 2 deals from cold outreach alone.

**2. Hire researchers, not deal sourcers**

Paid 3 researchers $800/month each.
Job: Find businesses matching my criteria. That's it.
Output: 80 qualified leads/week.
I just took meetings.

(Works from anywhere—mine were in India and Philippines.)

**3. Kill deals in 48 hours**

First call: 5 questions. If any answer is wrong, done.
- Why selling? (health/retirement = good, "tired" = red flag)
- Customer concentration? (>30% = pass)
- Owner involvement? (>30 hrs/week = pass)
- Clean books? (no accountant = pass)
- Asking multiple? (>4x = pass unless there's a story)

Most deals die on question 1.
That's the point.

**4. Speed is your moat**

LOI within 7 days of first call or you're wasting time.
I lost 3 deals by "taking my time."
Won 2 by moving faster than private equity.

Stop waiting for the perfect deal to find you.
The perfect deal is out there—being bought by someone faster.

**Your move:** Send 10 cold emails to business owners this week. Just 10. See what happens.

What's your best sourcing channel right now?

## Post 4: Why Most DD Advice Is Backwards

**Type:** Contrarian / Tactical
**Length:** ~400 words

---

I spent $18K on due diligence for a deal I should've killed in 15 minutes.

QoE report. Legal review. Tax analysis.
All to confirm what I felt on call #2: the seller was lying.

Most DD advice is backwards.

It says: "Be thorough. Check everything. Hire experts."

Here's what actually matters:

**Kill fast. Verify slow.**

Before you spend a dollar on lawyers:
→ Talk to 3 customers (call them, not email)
→ Ask for bank statements, not P&Ls (sellers can fake P&Ls in 20 minutes)
→ Visit the location unannounced (I found a "thriving" business half-empty)
→ Google the owner (found one with 2 lawsuits pending)

Do this in week 1. Costs you nothing.

**The expensive stuff comes AFTER you're 80% sure.**

QoE? Only if deal is >$500K.
Legal? Only after seller passes character tests.
Tax review? Only if structure is complex.

I've killed 40+ deals with a $0 DD process.
The ones that made it to paid DD? 90% closed.

DD isn't about finding problems.
It's about confirming what you already believe.

If you need DD to decide, you shouldn't be buying.

**Try this:** Next deal, do the $0 DD first. You'll save thousands.

What's your fastest deal-killer in DD?

## Post 5: First Acquisition Lessons

**Type:** Vulnerability / Lessons Learned
**Length:** ~420 words

---

My first acquisition almost bankrupted me.

Bought it anyway. Here's what I'd change.

**Mistake 1: Paid asking price**

Seller wanted $85K. I paid $85K.
Should've offered $60K.
"But he'll say no!" Yeah, or he'll counter at $72K.
I left $13K on the table because I was scared of a "no."

**Mistake 2: Trusted the seller's numbers**

He said $8K/month profit.
Reality: $4.2K. He was including his wife's "consulting" as a real expense.
Should've asked for bank statements, not spreadsheets.

**Mistake 3: No transition plan**

He left after 2 weeks.
Customers called. I had no idea who they were.
3 cancelled in month 1.
Should've demanded 90 days minimum.

**Mistake 4: Fixed what wasn't broken**

Immediately changed the website, logo, pricing.
Revenue dropped 15%.
Changed it back.
Should've run it untouched for 6 months first.

**Mistake 5: Went alone**

No advisor. No mentor. No community.
Every problem felt new.
Most had obvious solutions I couldn't see.

The business survived. Made money eventually.

But I turned a good deal into a hard deal by being impatient and ignorant.

First acquisition isn't about hitting a home run.
It's about not striking out.

What was your most expensive first-acquisition mistake?

## Post 6: Sourcely - The $4K Acquisition

**Type:** Case Study / Story
**Length:** ~500 words

---

I bought a SaaS product for $4,000 on Acquire.com.

18 months later, we're doing $40K ARR.

Here's everything I learned about micro acquisitions from the smallest deal I've ever done.

Most people think you need $500K+ to get into acquisition entrepreneurship.

Bullshit.

My first acquisition was Sourcely — an AI-powered academic research tool. $4K purchase price. 200 users. $400 MRR. One solo founder who built it as a side project and wanted out.

I almost passed on it. "Too small," I thought. "Not worth the time."

But something made me take the call. The founder was honest, the product worked, and the users loved it. So I wired $4K and became an acquisition entrepreneur.

**Lesson 1: Small deals are the best training ground.**

I made every mistake in the book. Bad onboarding. Broke the email system. Accidentally deleted the database (backups saved me). Took 3 months to figure out the codebase.

If this was a $500K deal, those mistakes would've been catastrophic. At $4K, they were tuition.

**Lesson 2: Technical debt is real.**

The codebase was a mess. No version control. Hardcoded API keys. A single file with 3,000 lines of JavaScript. I spent 6 months just refactoring.

But because revenue was low, I could take my time. Rebuilt the entire frontend, migrated to Supabase, set up proper DevOps. Now it's a product I'm proud of.

**Lesson 3: Small can still be profitable.**

We're doing $40K ARR now. Costs are $500/month (hosting + APIs). That's 87% margin. I work on it 5 hours a week.

ROI: 10x in 18 months. That beats most VC-backed startups.

**Lesson 4: Micro deals teach you the fundamentals.**

Customer support. Churn analysis. Pricing experiments. Marketing. Product roadmap. Hiring contractors.

All the same skills you need for a $5M acquisition, just at 1/1000th the scale and risk.

**Lesson 5: Start before you're ready.**

I had no idea what I was doing. No M&A experience. No SaaS operating experience. Just a belief that I could figure it out.

18 months later, I've done 2 more acquisitions, raised Pocket Fund, and have 10 analysts in India sourcing deals.

It all started with a $4K bet.

---

If you want to get into acquisitions but think you need $500K and a team, you're wrong.

Start with a $5K-$50K micro deal. Learn the fundamentals. Build confidence. Then scale up.

The best time to start was last year. The second best time is today.

What's stopping you from doing your first deal? Let me know in the comments.

## Post 7: Building Remote M&A Team in India

**Type:** Tactical / Behind-the-Scenes
**Length:** ~550 words

---

I built a micro PE firm with 10 analysts in India.

Cost: $15K/month total.

Output: 80 deals sourced, 12 LOIs, 2 closed acquisitions in 6 months.

Here's what nobody tells you about remote teams in M&A.

When I started Pocket Fund, everyone told me I needed to hire expensive analysts in the US.

"M&A is relationship-driven," they said. "You need people with pedigree."

I ignored them.

I hired 10 analysts in India. Average age: 24. Average experience: 2 years. Average cost: $1,500/month fully loaded.

Six months later, they've sourced 80+ deals, sent 12 LOIs, and helped close 2 acquisitions.

**What Actually Works:**

**1. Hire for hunger, not pedigree.**

None of my analysts have IIM or Ivy League degrees. Most come from tier-2 colleges. But every single one is hungry to learn, willing to grind, and eager to prove themselves.

That beats a lazy Harvard MBA every time.

**2. Processes > people.**

I documented everything. Deal sourcing scripts. Outreach templates. Financial modeling standards. CRM workflows. Scoring matrices.

When everything is a process, junior people can execute like seniors.

**3. Async communication is a superpower.**

India is 12 hours ahead. That means:
- They source deals while I sleep
- I review and give feedback while they sleep
- We're productive 20 hours a day

Async > synchronous.

**4. Specialize fast.**

Each analyst owns a vertical. One does SaaS. One does e-commerce. One does agencies. One handles outreach ops.

Specialists > generalists at this scale.

**5. Skin in the game.**

Every analyst gets deal carry. If we close an acquisition they sourced, they get 2-5% of our profit.

Suddenly, everyone cares about quality, not just quantity.

**What Doesn't Work:**

**1. Micromanagement.** I was on Slack 24/7 early on. Burnout hit in week 3. Now: Weekly syncs, clear OKRs, trust by default.

**2. Assuming context.** What's obvious to me isn't obvious to a 24-year-old analyst. I record Loom videos explaining every decision.

**3. Treating them like outsourced labor.** These aren't VAs. They're analysts building careers. I do 1-on-1s, teach M&A fundamentals, write recommendations.

**The Results:**

In 6 months:
- 80 deals sourced
- 12 LOIs sent
- 2 acquisitions closed
- Total cost: ~$90K

Compare that to 2 US analysts at $120K/year = $120K for 6 months.

I got 5x the output at 75% the cost.

---

You don't need expensive talent to do great work. You need clear processes, good training, and people who give a damn.

Remote teams in India, Philippines, or Latin America can absolutely compete in M&A. You just have to treat them like partners, not vendors.

Are you building with remote teams? What's working? Let's compare notes.

## Formatting Notes for LinkedIn

**Structure:**
- Hook in first 2 lines (what shows before "see more")
- Short paragraphs (2-3 lines max)
- Bullet points with bold headers
- Question at the end (drives comments)
- 5-8 hashtags at bottom

**What works on LinkedIn vs Twitter:**
- More story and context
- Can go deeper on lessons
- Carousels perform well
- Still need punchy hooks
- 1,300 chars ideal, 3,000 max
